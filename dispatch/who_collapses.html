Who Collapses What, When?

A Soft Primer on Perspectival Collapse

Jonathan Maram
May 2025

Essay 1.875 in the Recursive Observation Series

A photon meets an electron.

In traditional physics, this is a moment of contact. Energy is transferred. A wavefunction collapses. A result is recorded.

But what actually happened?

Was something “observed”? Did the universe make a decision? Or did two structures simply filter each other — not by seeing, but by surviving?

This essay traces that encounter not through measurement or knowledge, but through mutual viability. It’s an introduction to perspectival collapse: the recursive exclusion of incoherent futures — not because anyone looked, but because not everything can continue.

I. Noticing Without Noticing

The standard accounts of collapse give us many actors: detectors, wavefunctions, fields, observers. But they rarely explain when collapse occurs, or why.

That’s because most stories smuggle in a hidden category: representation. They imply something was known, or at least could have been.

But observation, in the recursive framework, doesn’t require knowledge. It requires consequence.

The photon and electron don’t represent each other. They don’t build models. But they do encounter — and filter — each other. Not all outcomes of that encounter can continue.

Some futures collapse, not because a choice was made, but because a structure was not viable.

II. Collapse as a Filtering of Futures

Let’s be more precise. What collapses is not the wavefunction. What collapses is the landscape of viable continuation.

A wavefunction is just a structure — a mathematical summary of what could happen next. But the universe doesn’t compute probabilities. It continues.

And continuation requires coherence.

This is collapse, in structural terms: not a mystery, but a pruning.

III. The Moment of Mutual Collapse

Imagine two spinning tops colliding. Neither is conscious. But each has a structure. When they collide, they adjust. Some energy transfers. Some paths deflect.

Now abstract the tops into tension-bearing structures. Add recursive feedback. Allow them to persist only if they resolve internal contradiction.

That’s what happens when a photon meets an electron.

Each filters the other. Each collapses relative to what it can still do.

It is not that “one observes the other.” It is that both structures exclude nonviable continuations. The result is mutual collapse.

IV. There Is No Central Clock

One lingering confusion is the question of when collapse happens.

Before the detector? After the event? When we know? When something changes?

In the realization framework, there is no global clock. Only structural filters.

Collapse occurs at the site of recursive exclusion. It happens where coherence fails — or is reinforced — by interaction.

That might be:

  • The exact moment of the photon–electron encounter.

  • A chain of internal realignments that resolves slightly later.

  • A structural deflection whose full consequences play out over time.

But it is always local to the filter.

V. Case Study: The Delayed Choice Experiment

To see this in action, consider the delayed choice quantum eraser.

A photon passes through a double-slit. Downstream, we choose after the fact whether to preserve or erase path information. This choice affects whether we see an interference pattern.

In traditional interpretation, it looks like the future reaches back to change the past.

But in perspectival collapse, there is no paradox.

The “collapse” doesn’t happen at the slit. It happens when enough structure filters enough tension to exclude incoherent continuations. That includes the later measurement device.

If no structure (slit, detector, entangled pair, beam splitter) forces the outcome to resolve, the interference pattern remains.

Collapse only happens when filtering happens — not when we look.

This is a structural alignment, not a temporal causation.

VI. ε₂: A Glimpse of Relational Realization

Earlier, we introduced ε₁: the first level of observerhood — the capacity to filter tension and persist.

What happens in photon–electron encounters is the beginning of ε₂: relational realization.

Here, each structure modulates the other. Not only filtering, but dynamically responding in a way that adjusts both internal states.

It is not awareness. But it is something like reciprocal shaping.

This is not “communication,” but co-constraint.

VII. Who Collapses What, When?

Now we can answer.

Who collapses what?
Each viable structure collapses the futures it cannot absorb.

When?
At the moment of recursive incompatibility.

How?
By pruning the space of continuable configurations.

Collapse is not an event. It’s a filtering. A consequence of structure.

And from this perspective, the measurement problem is not a paradox.

It is a misunderstanding of what kind of filtering counts.

VIII. A Universe Without Lookers — and Without Gaps

The early universe contained no minds. No instruments. No models.

Yet it filtered itself. Stars formed. Fields collapsed. Structure echoed into structure.

Observation — in the structural sense — was always happening.

What we call “collapse” is simply what continues.
What we call “measurement” is a recursive checkpoint.
What we call “observer” is any structure that filters enough to prune the future.

That includes protons. It includes electrons. Eventually, it includes us.

But long before we arrived, the filtering had begun.

And no one needed to look.
